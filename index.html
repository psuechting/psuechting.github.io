About Me

I am a researcher, data wrangler, and analyst working at the intersection of environmental studies, political economy, and climate policy. I hold a B.A. in Environmental Studies from Amherst College (2015) and an M.S. in Political Science from the University of Oregon (2022), where I am currently a Ph.D. Candidate in Environmental Studies, Science, and Policy, with an expected graduation in 2025/26.

My research sits at the confluence of energy transitions, climate policy, and social movements — spanning topics from sociotechnical regimes and carbon majors to U.S. electricity grid reform, transnational climate justice, and the politics of rural land use and transmission infrastructure. I have contributed to research projects on the social reception of energy in the developing world and on political and economic behavior in the oil and gas industry toward decarbonization, and co-authored a chapter on international environmental regimes in the Routledge Handbook of Global Environmental Politics (2022).

My career began in electoral and community organizing — directing canvass operations for Community Voters Project in Colorado and serving as State Operations Director for NextGen Climate in New Hampshire during the 2016 cycle. That work evolved into data and analytics, including a role as Data and Analysis Manager at Corporate Accountability, where I contributed to campaigns targeting GE, Philip Morris, and Exxon Mobil.

Beyond research, I bring over five years of experience in full-stack software development and CRM design and management, and specialize in informational systems design and implementation for mission-driven organizations. I currently serve as VP of Membership for the Graduate Teaching Fellows Federation (GTFF) Local 3544, AFT-OR/AFL-CIO, reflecting a longstanding commitment to academic labor organizing alongside my scholarly work.

I have been recognized with the Lokey Science Award from the UO Division of Graduate Studies, the Amherst Memorial Fellowship, and the Fredric Bastiat Fellowship from the Mercatus Center at George Mason University.
